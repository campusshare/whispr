Whistleblower Legal Guide

Know your rights before you report. This guide is educational, not legal advice.

Not legal advice. This guide is for educational purposes only. Whistleblowing laws vary by country and context. Consult a qualified attorney before taking action.

1. What Is Whistleblowing?

Whistleblowing is the act of exposing information about wrongdoing — corruption, abuse, fraud, safety violations — within an organization, institution, or government body. Whistleblowers serve a critical public interest function.

On this platform, we define whistleblowing as the submission of factual, evidence-backed accounts of misconduct in the public interest. Personal grievances, defamatory content, or unverified accusations do not qualify.

2. Your Rights As A Whistleblower

Protection from Retaliation
In many jurisdictions, laws prohibit employers from dismissing, demoting, or harassing employees who report wrongdoing in good faith. Document any retaliatory actions carefully.
Right to Anonymity
This platform is built to protect your identity. You are never required to identify yourself. Never upload documents that contain your name, employee ID, or other identifiers.
Good Faith Protection
If you report something you genuinely believe to be true, good-faith protection laws in many countries shield you from defamation claims, even if the report is later found to be inaccurate.
Right to Legal Counsel
Before approaching authorities or media, consult a lawyer specializing in employment or whistleblower law. Many offer pro-bono services for public interest cases.

3. Understanding the Risks

Real risks exist. Even with full anonymity on this platform, real-world actions you take outside this platform (talking to colleagues, using work devices, printing documents) can expose you.

Legal action: Organizations may attempt civil lawsuits even against anonymous reporters. Courts can compel platforms to reveal user identity — but we have no identity to reveal.
Employment consequences: Despite protections, retaliation is common. Prepare for the possibility of employment consequences before reporting.
Digital forensics: Documents may have invisible watermarks or metadata. Screenshot all documents rather than uploading originals.
Social engineering: Bad actors may attempt to contact you to identify you. Never reveal details that could narrow down who you are.

4. Before You Report

  • Consult a whistleblower attorney if possible
  • Understand what protections apply in your specific country and sector
  • Document all evidence on a device not connected to your employer's network
  • Do not share your intention to report with colleagues, friends, or family unless you trust them completely
  • Consider whether an internal report was previously filed and ignored — this strengthens your case
  • Back up your evidence in multiple secure locations before submitting

5. Handling Evidence Safely

Our platform automatically strips EXIF metadata from uploaded files. However, you should take precautions before uploading:

  • Screenshots over originals — Screenshot documents rather than uploading original files which may contain invisible watermarks
  • Personal devices only — Never use work computers, phones, or networks to access this platform
  • No VPN on work network — Using a VPN on a corporate network is still detectable
  • Blur faces and identifiers — Use a photo editor to redact identifying information before upload, even though our AI also scans for faces
  • Public wifi with caution — For maximum security, use a mobile data connection on a personal device not registered to you

6. If You Face Retaliation

If you experience retaliation after reporting, document everything:

  • Keep records of all communications (emails, messages) related to the retaliation
  • Note dates, times, and witnesses to verbal harassment or threats
  • File a formal complaint with relevant labor authorities
  • Contact an employment lawyer immediately
  • Contact journalist or NGO partners through our secure messaging system

7. Ghana-Specific Protections

Ghana has enacted whistleblower legislation. The sections below are summaries only — verify current law with a qualified attorney.

Whistleblower Act, 2006 (Act 720)
Provides protection for persons who disclose information about wrongdoing in the public interest. Criminalizes retaliation against whistleblowers. Establishes a reward fund for disclosures leading to recovery of public funds.
Office of the Special Prosecutor (OSP)
Established to investigate corruption and prosecute public officials. Can receive anonymous tips. Has independent prosecutorial power outside the Attorney General's office.
CHRAJ (Commission on Human Rights)
Can receive whistleblower complaints and has investigative powers. Protects against violations of fundamental human rights including retaliation.
